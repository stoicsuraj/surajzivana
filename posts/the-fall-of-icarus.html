People deem Icarus greedy for height, as if he reached for something he never should have touched. But do people truly agree with that?

His father crafted wings to escape captivity, fastening feathers with wax, warning him not to fly too close to the sun nor too near the ocean. The wax would melt in the sun's blaze, and the feathers would grow heavy if soaked by the sea. Knowing every limitation, Icarus still chose to fly. He rose from the island as the Greeks watched, their eyes fixed upon him. He flew. He felt the wind against his skin, the rush of Eleuthera — freedom. And he flew higher.

Was it truly his mistake? Icarus was a mortal, one of us. Though the Greeks spoke often of gods and hubris, Icarus himself was no Olympian. He was human. We like to retell his fall as a lesson, but perhaps it was simply a confession of our own nature. As he ascended, desire grew within him. Call it greed, call it hunger, call it philotimo, the fierce pride of proving oneself.

Why should a person not want more?

We mock Icarus for his folly, yet we speak from the comfort of the twenty-first century, safe behind glowing screens. It is easy to judge a boy in the sky when our own risks are so calculated. We are jealous. We are ambitious. There is no denying it. The more we conquer, the more we crave.

First school, then high school. Life will begin after college. Or perhaps after a master's degree. Should I pursue a doctorate? And once I hold a doctorate, why not devote my life to research until I die? The hunger does not end. The desire to exceed what we already have is written into our psyche.

And what if Icarus had flown too low instead? The ocean would have claimed him all the same. The feathers would have grown heavy, and he would have drowned quietly, safely, unnoticed. Is that not another tragedy? To possess potential and never dare to test it. To fear the sun so much that you never leave the shoreline. The universe asks for balance. It whispers metron ariston, moderation is best. But humans are not static creatures. We are dynamic to the core. The moment we reach stability, the urge for more unsettles us again. Empires have risen and fallen because someone wanted just a little more sky.

And yet I once heard someone say that Icarus laughed as he fell. Why should he not? He had touched the sky. He had felt the warmth of Helios on his face. He had lived, if only for a moment, beyond the ordinary limits of men. Perhaps it was excessive. Perhaps it was hubris. But it was alive. Another person told me that he had never seen a dying man whose eyes were not full of life. Maybe that is the true tragedy. We all know when to begin. We rarely know when to stop. And perhaps that is what makes us human.
